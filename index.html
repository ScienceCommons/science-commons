About

Curate-Science's mission is to accelerate the growth of cumulative knowledge and innovation by organizing and curating key information about scientific articles, including consolidating follow-up independent replications, available data/syntax and experimental materials, post-publication comments, and publication bias indices.

As much as possible, the information will be automatically aggregated via APIs and text mining from pre-existing sources, for example, raw data/syntax and materials aggregated from Figshare.com, Datadryad.org, and the OpenScienceFramework.org and post-pub comments consolidated from PubMedCommons, PubPeer.com, and PLoS and Frontiers journals. Information regarding follow-up independent replications will be incorporated via crowd-sourcing and automatic aggregation.

Organizing such information for each scientific article has numerous benefits including facilitating the critique, evaluation, extension, application, analysis, re-analysis, reproducibility, and interpretation of research findings. Organizing this information will also facilitate the execution of independent replications and yield new theoretical insights via more sophisticated forms of real-time publication-bias-corrected meta-analyses.

Core Features

Consolidated Independent Replication Info

Our flagship feature is the consolidation, organization, and curation of follow-up independent replications. This includes dynamically updating graphs that easily allows you to compare the replication effect sizes with the original study effect size.

Available Data/Syntax, Materials, & Pre-Registration

Available raw data sets, syntax files, experimental materials, and pre-registration protocols will be easily accessible at the study-level. For R syntax files, users will be able to execute analyses in-browser.

Crowd Curation via Editable Fields

Our web application will feature editable fields so that registered users can add, modify, or update information related to independent replications, data files, syntax files, experimental materials, pre-registration, and disclosure information. Edits will be tracked via revision history.

Color-Coded Badge System

To facilitate the search and discovery of scientific articles, we use a color-coded badge system -- inspired by COS' open-science badges -- that depicts key article characteristics such as replicability, available data, reproducible analyses, available materials, methods disclosure, and pre-registration.

Element-specific Post-Publication Comments

Users will be able to add post-publication comments directed toward specific elements of an article, including in-line commenting and comment-helpfulness up- and down-voting. We will also aggregate and display pre-existing post-pub comments from various external sources.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Who is the intended audience for Curate-Science?
A: Our primary audience is the community of academic, government, and industry researchers. However, we are designing the website so that the organized information is also useful to students, educators, journalists, and public policy makers. For example, a journalist could look up an article to see whether findings have been independently corroborated and/or whether critical flaws or limitations have been identified so that they can write a more balanced news article about a published article.

Q: What is curation?
 A: Digital curation is the process of selecting, filtering, and extracting information as to increase its quality. Curate-Science organizes, selects, filters, and extracts information from a diverse set of sources with the goal of increasing the quality of fundamental information about scientific articles.

Q: Who can access and consume information about articles on Curate-Science?
 A: Anyone, including non-registered users, can lookup information on Curate-Science.

Q: Who can add, modify, and update article information on Curate-Science?
 A: Only registered users will be able to add, modify, and update information about articles. That being said, anyone that is affiliated with a research organization can become a registered user, as long as they provide their real names, email address, affiliation, and title (e.g., post-doc, graduate student, undergraduate student, research assistant).

Q: How will you ensure quality control of the information posted about articles on Curate-Science?
 A: We will employ a two-stage verification process for some of the information whereby information initially posted will be labeled as "unverified" until a second user confirms it, at which time it will appear as verified. This will be the case for key statistics, independent replication information, and publication bias indices. Like Wikipedia, we will also have a revision history for each editable/updatable field showing which user changed what information on what date (and any notes regarding the edit left by the user).

Q: Will I be able to post data/materials directly on an article page on Curate-Science?
 A: No, not at the moment. Our current focus is to organize and curate the available data/materials that are already hosted by the many public repositories that already exist. However, in our quest to radically simplify the fundamental scientific practice of sharing data/materials, we may consider this option in the future.

Q: Will I be able to post unpublished manuscripts on Curate-Science?
 A: Eventually yes. Our initial focus is on organizing and curating information for published articles, however, we will eventually allow researchers to post working papers with corresponding data/syntax and materials files as a way to significantly facilitate the pre-publication peer review approach that has been so successful in physics and mathematics (i.e., arxiv.org).

Q: Is it really feasible to organize and curate information for all scientific articles? In other words, why would researchers be willing to spend their precious time curating information on Curate-Science?
 A: Our view is that expert researchers should be highly motivated to add and update information regarding published articles in their own area of research because there is an intrinsic interest to update the scientific record to more accurately reflect the totality of the evidence. Hence, we do not expect researchers to curate random articles on Curate-Science, but rather to add/modify information to articles in their own area of research (e.g., when flawed or unreliable findings enter the literature).

Q: What is Science-Commons' business model?
 A: We will soon be incorporating as a non-profit organization. Curate-Science organizes and curates fundamental information about unpublished and published scientific articles and we see this information as a public good that belongs to the community of scientists. Consequently, information organized on Curate-Science is and will forever remain a free public good accessible to anyone with an Internet connection.

Q: Who is funding Curate-Science?
 A: We are currently applying for funding from the Sloan Foundation, Templeton Foundation, Knight Foundation, Shuttleworth Foundation, and Open Society Foundation.

Q: Is Curate-Science associated with the Open Science Framework hosted by the Center for Open Science?
 A: No. Though we will be linking available data, materials, and pre-registration information from the Open Science Framework, we are an independent group of researchers in no way formally associated with the Open Science Framework.

Q: What is an "alpha release" and what does that mean in Curate-Science's case?
 A: An "alpha release" is a stage of software development whereby a product is in its early stages of development and may be unstable, contain errors, and not have all product features yet implemented. In our case, this means the version of Curate-Science you are currently viewing is a very rough prototype that only shows some of the website's features (most of our core features are currently being developed; see the Features page for more information). The actual website will offer all of our features including a blazingly fast and advanced search, an innovative search results page, the ability for registered users to add, modify, and update article information (and revision history for all changes), and component-specific in-line commenting. The actual website will also have a completely different look, identity, and branding; we are currently working with a design firm carefully designing an elegant and intuitive user interface to allow for a pleasant user experience.

Q: When will I be able to add, modify, and update information on Curate-Science?
 A: Registered users will be able to add/modify information once we launch our Beta (a "Beta" is a stage of software development where a product has implemented all of its core features and is ready for testing by the community of users). Our projected Beta launch is May-June 2014. You can sign up here to be automatically notified when our Beta website version is ready.
