About

Replicability is a cornerstone of science yet independent replication information is not currently organized in a systematic fashion, making it difficult to gauge the reliability of empirical findings. Independent verification of data and analyses is another hallmark of science, yet in practice verification is extremely challenging because available data and syntax are difficult to track down.

Curate-Science aims to solve both of these problems by providing an online platform that substantially facilitates these two crucial processes of science. At the article level, we will automatically aggregate available independent replication information and data/syntax files from pre-existing sources (e.g., from PsychFileDrawer.org, Figshare.com, and the OpenScienceFramework.org). Critically, however, registered users will also be able to add, modify, and update such information, including in-line commenting directed at specific elements of an article (e.g., data/syntax problem, statistical reporting question, etc.). For articles with available R syntax files, users will also be able to execute in-browser analyses, which will go a long way in facilitating the independent verification process so crucial to science.

Our vision is that building such a platform will accelerate the growth of cumulative knowledge and innovation by facilitating and incentivizing independent replications and verification, but also by facilitating the post-publication critique, evaluation, extension, re-analysis, and interpretation of findings (see Features, for a list of our core features).

Core Features

Consolidated Independent Replication Info

 Our flagship feature is the consolidation, organization, and curation of follow-up independent replications for published articles. This includes dynamically updating graphs that easily allows you to compare the replication effect sizes with the original study effect size.

Available Data/Syntax, Materials, & Pre-Registration

 We will also consolidate available raw data sets, syntax files, experimental materials, and pre-registration protocols at the study-level for published articles. For R syntax files, users will be able to execute in-browser analyses in order to independently verify the reproducibility of analyes.

Crowd Curation via Editable Fields

 Our web application will feature editable fields so that registered users can add, modify, or update information related to independent replications, data files, syntax files, experimental materials, pre-registration, and disclosure information. Edits will be tracked via revision history.

Component Indicator Icon System

 To facilitate the search and discovery of scientific articles, we will use an indicator system -- inspired by the Open Science Collaboration's open-science badges -- that depicts key article components such as replicability information, available data, reproducibility, available materials, methods disclosure, and pre-registration.

Element-specific Post-Publication Comments

 Users will be able to add post-publication comments about specific elements of an article, including in-line commenting and comment-helpfulness up-down voting. We will also aggregate and display pre-existing post-pub comments from various external sources (e.g., from PubMedCommons, PubPeer.com, etc.).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Who is Curate-Science's intended audience?
A: Our primary audience is the community of academic, government, and industry researchers. However, we are designing the website so that the organized information is also useful to students, educators, journalists, and public policy makers. For example, a journalist could look up an article to see whether findings have been independently corroborated and/or whether critical flaws or limitations have been identified so that they can write a more balanced news article about a published article.

Q: What is curation?
 A: Digital curation is the process of selecting, filtering, and extracting information as to increase its quality. Curate-Science organizes, selects, filters, and extracts information from a diverse set of sources with the goal of increasing the quality of fundamental information about scientific articles.

Q: Who can access and consume information about articles on Curate-Science?
 A: Anyone, including non-registered users, can lookup information on Curate-Science.

Q: Who can add, modify, and update article information on Curate-Science?
 A: Only registered users will be able to add, modify, and update information about articles. That being said, anyone that is affiliated with a research organization can become a registered user, as long as they provide their real names, email address, affiliation, and title (e.g., post-doc, graduate student, undergraduate student, research assistant).

Q: How will you ensure quality control of the information posted about articles on Curate-Science?
 A: We will employ a two-stage verification process for some of the information whereby information initially posted will be labeled as "unverified" until a second user confirms it, at which time it will appear as verified. This will be the case for key statistics, independent replication information, and publication bias indices. Like Wikipedia, we will also have a revision history for each editable/updatable field showing which user changed what information on what date (and any notes regarding the edit left by the user).

Q: Will I be able to post data/materials directly on an article page on Curate-Science?
 A: No, not at the moment. Our current focus is to organize and curate the available data/materials that are already hosted by the many public repositories that already exist. However, in our quest to radically simplify the fundamental scientific practice of sharing data/materials, we may consider this option in the near future.

Q: Will I be able to post unpublished manuscripts on Curate-Science?
 A: Eventually yes. Our initial focus is on organizing and curating information for published articles, however, we will eventually allow researchers to post working papers with corresponding data/syntax and materials files as a way to significantly facilitate the pre-publication peer review approach that has been so successful in physics and mathematics (i.e., arxiv.org).

Q: Is it really feasible to organize and curate information for all scientific articles? In other words, why would researchers be willing to spend their precious time curating information on Curate-Science?
 A: Our view is that researchers should be highly motivated to add and update information regarding published articles in their own area of research because there is an intrinsic interest to update the scientific record to more accurately reflect the totality of the evidence. We also expect -- just like what happened with Wikipedia -- that more prominent and/or controversial articles will be curated first given that a large number of researchers are interested in these articles. Other articles will likely be curated commensurate to the level of interest commanded by the market, though of course article authors are free to curate their own articles as much as they want (and for good reasons, e.g. available data citation advantage, see Piwowar & Vision, 2013)

Q: What is Curate-Science's business model?
 A: We will soon be incorporating as a non-profit organization. Curate-Science organizes and curates fundamental information about unpublished and published scientific articles and we see this information as a public good that belongs to the community of scientists. Consequently, information organized on Curate-Science is and will forever remain a free public good accessible to anyone with an Internet connection.

Q: Who is funding Curate-Science?
 A: We are currently applying for funding from the Sloan Foundation, Templeton Foundation, Knight Foundation, Shuttleworth Foundation, and Open Society Foundation.

Q: Is Curate-Science associated with the Open Science Framework hosted by the Center for Open Science?
 A: No. Though we will be linking available data, materials, and pre-registration information from the Open Science Framework, we are an independent group of researchers in no way formally associated with the Open Science Framework.

Q: How is Curate-Science different from Figshare.com, DataDryad.org, and Harvard's Dataverse?
 A: Figshare.com, DataDryad.org, and Harvard's Dataverse (and many other similar websites) are data repositories where researchers can make their data, syntax, and materials publicly availalbe and get credit for doing so. Curate-Science organizes, consolidates, and curates all of this publicly available information from as many different sources as possible at the study-level for all published articles with a DOI. Curate-science also provides a platform for the crowd to verify analyses and post comments regarding specific issues such as reproducibility of analyses, problems with posted materials/stimuli, etc.

Q: How is Curate-Science different from the Open Science Framework?
 A: The Open Science Framework is a place for researchers to archive, collaborate on, and pre-register their research projects to facilitate researchers' workflow to help increase the alignment between scientific values and scientific practices (see more details on OSF's about page). In contrast, Curate-Science, as its name implies, is focused primarily on the curation of scientific information tied to published articles by providing a platform for users to add, modify, update, and comment on published article's replication and reproducibility information (among other things).

Q: What is an "alpha release" and what does that mean in Curate-Science's case?
 A: An "alpha release" is a stage of software development whereby a product is in its early stages of development and may be unstable, contain errors, and not have all product features yet implemented. In our case, this means the version of Curate-Science you are currently viewing is a very rough prototype that only shows some of the website's features (most of our core features are currently being developed; see the Features page for more information). The actual website will offer all of our core features including a blazingly fast and advanced search, an innovative search results page, the ability for registered users to add, modify, and update article information (and revision history for all changes), and component-specific in-line commenting. The actual website will have a completely different look, identity, and branding; we are currently working with a design firm carefully designing an elegant and intuitive user interface to allow for a pleasant user experience.

Q: When will I be able to add, modify, and update information on Curate-Science?
 A: Registered users will be able to add/modify information once we launch our Beta (a "Beta" is a stage of software development where a product has implemented all of its core features and is ready for testing by the community of users). Our projected Beta launch is May-June 2014.
